# Down to the Seep

The slope is all glare and hard light.
Even under the trees the heat presses like a hand on the back of your neck, and the leaves above you seem to burn where the sun reaches them.

You find the wolverine under a stand of rock-broken roots, hurt and nearly spent, one flank torn and its breath gone shallow with thirst.
It bares its teeth when you approach, but the gesture has more pride left in it than strength.

You know there is still water lower down, where the valley floor narrows and a little seep comes slowly out from the stones.
No creature in this state will reach it alone.

So you tear strips from your cloak, bind what you can with crushed red leaves and the sticky juice of heat-hardy plants, and haul the wolverine up across your shoulders before it can object in any meaningful way.

The way down is cruel.
Twice you nearly lose your footing. Once the beast twists and almost throws you.
Still you carry it, step after step, until the rocks grow cool enough to touch and the first thread of water shows itself glistening in the shade below.

There you lower it gently and let it drink.
The wolverine rests, then rises again with a look in its eyes that is no longer only pain.
