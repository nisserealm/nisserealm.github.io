# Under the Dry Roots

The heat has gone past discomfort and become a presence of its own.
Leaves hang dull and curled, and every step through the forest raises the smell of dust, bark, and old sun-baked needles.

You notice the sign before you see the creature: a few quills caught in dry moss, then a little hollow under exposed roots where the shade still holds.

There a porcupine lies half-curled, too hot and thirsty to flee quickly, though not too weak to watch you with sharp suspicion.

You do not crowd it.
You settle a little way off, scrape a hollow in the dry earth, and pour a careful little measure of water there instead of offering your hand.

The porcupine waits a long time before shuffling toward it.
Then, when the thirst is stronger than fear, it drinks.

You gather broad cooling leaves from the darkest patch of shade you can find and lay them nearby, not touching the creature, only making the place easier for it to bear.

That is enough.
