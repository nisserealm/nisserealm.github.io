# Before the Hawk Came Down

The meadow is waking, but danger wakes early too.
Somewhere beyond the mist comes the hard frantic cry of something small that knows death is already circling.

You run toward the sound and come upon a fox cub tangled in a loop of old snare-cord at the edge of the field, where a trapper must have set it for easier prey.
Above, a hawk is already wheeling lower, reading the helpless movement below.

You do not stop to think yourself into caution.
You seize the cord, slash it loose against a sharp stone, and throw your cloak wide just as the hawk dives.

The bird veers away at the last instant in a blast of wings and anger.
The cub snaps at you from fear, then falters when one foreleg gives under it. The snare has cut deep enough to leave it weak.

You bind the worst of the hurt with torn cloth and field-plant pulp, fast and rough but good enough to carry it through the morning.
Then you sit back on your heels and let the cub decide whether you are another danger or the one that broke the first.

When it finally comes close, it does so with all the fierce suspicion a fox can manage.
