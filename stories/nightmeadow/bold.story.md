# Eyes Round the Tree

You are nearly asleep beneath the tree when the meadow changes its breathing.
The grass no longer whispers evenly. Something is circling, then pausing, then circling again.

You rise at once and see them in the dark: lean meadow hunters, coyote-thin and hungry, working their way inward toward a shallow scrape in the roots.
There, pressed against the trunk, is a young badger, too small to stand against a whole ring of teeth and patience.

You do not wait for the night to make up its mind.
You snatch a burning branch from your little banked fire, shout into the dark, and charge straight at the nearest pair of eyes.

The coyotes break wide, circle again, and then think better of the bargain when you do not yield a step.
One comes close enough to taste your anger and leaves with a yelp for its trouble.

Only when the ring finally dissolves do you turn back to the small badger.
It is bloodied around one shoulder and shaking with more rage than fear.

You kneel in front of it as if before a lord who has had a very bad night, mash bitter root and meadow leaves into a strong-smelling pulp, and set the paste near enough for the beast to take or refuse on its own terms.
It lets you help, which is not the same thing as gentleness, but is close enough for the dark hour.
