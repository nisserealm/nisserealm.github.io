# Moon Between the Antlers

You pass through the night forest with the same gentleness you would show by day.
You step around sleeping frogs in the wet grass, ease a thorn from a rabbit track, and leave the low flowering plants unbroken though the path would be easier if you pushed straight through them.

At the edge of a moonlit clearing you find a young deer standing on three legs.
One foreleg is cut and trembling, not from a deep wound but from a sharp stone or snag hidden somewhere in the dark.

You do not rush it.
You sit in the grass where it can see you, speak softly, and wait until its fear grows less urgent than its pain.

When at last it allows you near, you gather cool moss and two narrow healing leaves from the edge of the clearing, crush them in your palm until their juice runs, and lay the green wetness over the hurt.

The deer shivers once, then stands more easily.
It lowers its head toward you, breathes against your shoulder, and remains there in the moonlight as if listening to a promise neither of you needed to speak.
