# Iron in the Underbrush

You smell iron before you see the trap.
Not smithy iron, but the mean cold scent of worked metal hidden under leaves, set for a creature valuable enough to be sold in town by someone willing to wound the forest to get paid.

Then comes the sound: a low furious yowl, ragged with pain.

You find the tomcat in a cruel leg trap beneath a bramble, all black fur, yellow eyes, and bared teeth.
The brush around it is broken, and not far off you spot the marks of boots and a dragged sack. Whoever set the trap meant to come back.

You do not hesitate.
You wrench a branch free, hammer the jaws of the trap until the spring slips, and tear the iron apart enough to pull the cat clear before the trapper can return.

The wound is ugly, and the beast would never suffer your touch if you came at it meekly.
So you work like something born of the same forest anger it carries: fast, certain, unapologetic.

You mash bitter leaves and dark resin between two stones and press the sharp-smelling juice around the torn flesh while the cat curses you in every way a cat can curse a living soul.
When it finally realizes you are not another captor, the sound changes.

You break the trap to pieces and fling the iron into the streambed stones.

It follows when you go, not with obedience, but with the proud decision of something that has judged you dangerous to the right enemies.
