# Still Among the Ferns

The forest grows deeper and dimmer until even your own footfall seems too loud.
So you slow down.

You begin to move the way deer move: pausing often, listening longer than seems necessary, letting the wood accept you one breath at a time.

At a little clearing ringed with ferns, you finally see it: a deer standing half in light, half in shade, so still it might have been carved from the hour itself.

You do not call out. You do not reach.
You lower your gaze, kneel by the moss, and wait until the forest no longer feels disturbed by your presence.

Minutes pass. Then the deer steps forward and noses your shoulder as if testing whether you are truly as quiet-hearted as you seem.

By dusk that distance is gone.
