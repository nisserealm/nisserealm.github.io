# The Tree That Remembered

You pass through the deep forest with a gentle hand.
You scatter crumbs for a squirrel on a stone, step around frogs in the moss, and take care not to break the low branches that seem almost to bow over the path.

By evening you are tired in the quiet, inward way that feels too close to loneliness.
At last you come to a great old oak and rest against its roots, letting your head fall back against the trunk as if the tree itself might keep you company for a while.

You lie there in the lap of the roots and say nothing.
After a little while butterflies begin to settle around you, on the bark, on the moss, even in your hair, as though the tree has decided you are safe enough to hold for a moment.

One does not fly off again.
It stays near your brow until its wings grow too bright and too delicate for any true butterfly, and then you see that it is a tiny being with a face no larger than a thumbprint, watching you with grave, bright curiosity.

"You were kind to all the little lives on the path," she says. "So the forest was kind enough to let me find you."

You do not feel lonely after that.
