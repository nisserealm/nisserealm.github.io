# The Donkey in the Lane

Morning lies broad over the cultivated land.
Beyond the fields run little tracks between hedges and stones, with here and there a willow, a ditch, or a low place where water gathers after rain.

You do not stride down the road as if it were empty.
You stop often, study the marks in the dust, and let your eyes move farther than your feet.

That is how you notice the drag-marks first, and then the donkey itself farther on in a narrow lane, standing with one torn tether still hanging from its neck.
Its coat is burr-caught, its flank raw where rope has rubbed, and there is no one coming after it.

You do not chase it.
You cut fennel from the verge, speak low, and take the longer curve around so the lane itself turns the animal toward you instead of your hands forcing the matter.

The donkey watches you sidelong for a long while, weighing your patience.
At last it comes close enough for you to work the torn rope free and rub crushed plantain over the rubbed skin beneath it.

Later, at the edge of the next field, an old woman tells you the beast has belonged to no one since spring and has haunted the lanes ever since, too gentle to thrive and too stubborn to die.
