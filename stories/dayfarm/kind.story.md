# The Dog by the Chapel Track

The day is kind and clear, with people out among the strips of ploughed earth and kitchen gardens.
Near a little roadside chapel, where the path turns toward the fields, you find offerings of flowers, a clay lamp gone cold, and the faint sound of an animal trying not to whine.

You set down a little crust beside the stone step, speak softly into the shade, and wait until the creature hidden there believes your quiet.

It is a dog, thin-ribbed and dusty, with one forepaw split between the pads and burr thick in its coat.
Someone has tied a strip of worn cloth round its neck once, long ago, but it hangs loose and tells you nothing except that it has been near people and lost them.

You work the burr free strand by strand, rub the split paw with crushed plantain and lamb's-ear, and soften bread in a little water from the chapel spring.
The dog eats, then presses its brow against your wrist with a gratitude too quiet for barking.

An old caretaker from the chapel garden sees this and tells you the dog has slept there since its master died on the road weeks ago.
"If it follows you," she says, "let it. Better a road than waiting for ghosts."
