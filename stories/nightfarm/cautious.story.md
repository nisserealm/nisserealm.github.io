# Eyes Along the Hedge

Night in cultivated country is never truly still.
Somewhere a dog barks, somewhere water moves against stone, and the hedges along the lanes seem to hold more eyes than daylight ever admits.

You mean only to pass quietly by, but a sour unease in the dark makes you slow your steps.
So you keep to the hedge instead of the road and listen.

That is how you hear the breathing first.
Not a thief, not a fox, but a dog crouched low beneath hawthorn with a cruel wire loop still hanging from its neck, as though someone meant to take it and sell it at first light.

You do not lunge for it.
You settle into the grass, murmur to it from where you are, and let the dark do what the dark does best: make patience easier than force.

When at last it crawls closer, you cut the wire with the little knife you keep in your boot and lay nettle-salve and soft moss over the skin cut raw beneath it.
The dog leans against your knee only once, but that is enough.
