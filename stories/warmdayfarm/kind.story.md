# Water at the Creek Bend

The backroads are parched by afternoon.
Dust hangs in the air, the field grass has gone pale, and even the creek at the bend runs so low you can see half its stones dry in the sun.

You stop there first, not because it is your road, but because any living thing would need the place before the day is done.

Under the elder shade nearby lies a dog too worn to bark and too proud to beg.
It watches while you clear a little hollow in the mud, help the water gather cleanly there, and lay broad leaves over the hottest stones so the patch will keep cool a little longer.

Only after that do you kneel.
The dog comes near enough for you to see the cracked pads of its feet and the dryness at its nose.
You let it drink from your hands before the stirred mud can cloud the water, then rub goose-fat and herb salve into the sore places and share the last piece of apple from your pocket.

Two children gathering kindling tell you the dog belonged to a holding that burned in last year's dry wind.
It has haunted the bend ever since, following no one home.
