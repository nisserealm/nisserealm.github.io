# Shade by the Thistles

The day is so warm that even the flies sound lazy.
Heat lies over the cultivated country in wavering sheets, and the open road gives nothing away except dust and glare.

So you keep to hedges and field banks where signs hold longer and the ground is cooler underfoot.

Near a boundary stone half-hidden in thistles, you find a donkey standing in the little scrap of shade cast by the hedge.
One forehoof is lifted clear of the ground, and when it shifts you hear the small hard catch of pain.

You do not go straight for the leg.
You stay beside it until its breathing settles, then lay a hand along the shoulder, work the hoof gently into your grip, and draw out the thorn lodged deep in the soft edge of it.

You press chewed plantain over the place and wait until the shaking passes.
Only then do you notice the worn mark around its neck and the rag of old tether still caught in the hair there.

An orchard keeper coming down the lane tells you the beast has been wandering between fields for days since a trader cast it loose rather than lead a limping animal any farther.
"If it has taken to you," he says, "that is better luck than most beasts get."

The donkey touches your shoulder with its muzzle, almost shyly.
